Coal between the 1850s up until the 1950s was essential, and the highest consumed energy source for America at the time. Coal was one of the major foundations of industrialization throughout the 19ᵗʰ century and its use over petroleum stayed higher by around 10~13 Quadrillion British Thermal Units through the period highlighted in red in the graph. Coal's drop suffered at around 1940 was due to a massive coal miner strike with 500,000 miners in April 1943 due to unfair conditions despite President Roosevelt's attempts to make miners adhere to a “ no-strike” period during the Second World War.

With a rising need for coal in the US, it came with a need of miners and mines. Colorado Fuel & Iron company (or CF&I), owned by John D. Rockefeller Junior, set up establishments in Denver and Pueblo in 1892. His practices however, were questionable under the Colorado law. They purposely hired as many foreign workers as they were able to. In the companies eyes, if they couldn't speak the same language, it would be harder for them to find a common goal and unite.

Coal mines in Colorado ended up using mice instead of canary to detect carbon monoxide in the air. Mice's natural good hearing allowed them to be able to hear timber starting to snap or a cave in was imminent.

To a miner there were countless dangers in a mine. As coal is decaying, it releases methane gas in the process, and with clouds of coal dust in the mine not only sinking into miners lungs, when in contact with a heat source it creates an explosion. Methane gas can build up in certain places, and if there is enough gas as well as it coming in contact with a heat source, it explodes. If miners were able to survive mine explosions, it would release new carbon monoxide into the air, an event called “after damp” (see ad to the right), which would kill the majority of miners if they didn't die by suffocation.
