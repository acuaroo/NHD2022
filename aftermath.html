Stove and ruins at the tent colony

After the Ludlow massacre, the event immediately captivated the American public, exposing flaws and consequences of unchecked company power. Colorado Governor Ammons and Frederick Farrar (the attorney general who seeked punishment of the strikers) were both swept out of office.

A memorium was created by the United Mine Workers of America in 1918. Engrained on the memorial is the text

“In Memory of the Men, Women and Children Who Lost Their Lives in Freedom's Cause at Ludlow, Colorado, April 20, 1914.”

Political cartoon about Rockefeller's reaction to the Ludlow massacre, ULS Digital Collections

This cartoon shows mine workers in a good light, simply asking for a company union then afterwards coming out of “LUDLOW” with their children dead, and Rockefeller defending himself and his company in front of a congressional committee.

Miners' wants for a company union and better pay were ultimately ignored, instead, Rockefeller attempted to work with professionals to better improve company towns. In the end little has changed the way mines were operated.
